A Region in Retreat: Press Freedom Across the Americas

The 2026 scores from Reporters Without Borders show press freedom declining across the Americas, with over half of the region's countries losing ground.

Ava Gough July 2026

This past April, Reporters Without Borders (RSF), an independent non-profit that measures global press freedom, announced that it has reached its lowest point in the 25 years the organization has published its annual World Press Freedom Index.

In its review of this year's findings, RSF expressed particular concern for press freedom across the Americas, which has fallen 14 points as a region since 2022, a shift the organization largely attributes to political actors and organized crime.

How RSF defines press freedom

"Press freedom is defined as the ability of journalists as individuals and collectives to select, produce, and disseminate news in the public interest independent of political, economic, legal, and social interference and in the absence of threats to their physical and mental safety."

At first glance, the region still appears to be in a relatively steady state compared to others, coming in second only to Europe/Balkans in average press freedom scores. However, a look at its countries on an individual level reveals stark differences between their numbers.

How the region compares globally

Despite ranking second overall in terms of average scores across regions, the Americas fall behind Europe/Balkans by more than 18 points, while only surpassing Africa by 2.27 points.

The region is also much closer in score to those in fourth and fifth place, earning just 7.63 more points than the Asia-Pacific and 16 points more than Eastern Europe - Central Asia (EECA).

At the same time, the difference between the Americas and the lowest scoring region, Middle East and North Africa (MENA), is nearly the same as the gap it shares with Europe/Balkans, highlighting how its regional ranking obscures its weakening state of press freedom.

The region's fragility is also reflected in its declining average over the past 12 months. Since RSF's 2025 index, its press freedom score dropped by 1.08 points, surpassed only by EECA, which fell by 1.48 points. Every other region that declined lost less than 1 point from their average score, further reinforcing the significance of the Americas downward trend.

Where Press Freedom Lost Ground

In total, press freedom has fallen in around 60% of the region's countries, a majority of which are located in South America.

Of the 28 countries in the Americas, including OECS, Ecuador's press freedom score has declined the most. Since 2025, it has dropped nearly 10 points, while the Dominican Republic dropped the least compared to those whose scores have fallen, declining by 0.14 points.

Out of the countries whose press freedom has increased in the last year, Uruguay has made the most progress, improving by 3.54 points, followed by Cuba, whose score went up by 3.19 points.

Canada made the least progress of the countries that improved, earning just 0.01 points since 2025, but still takes first place in overall scores across the Americas. The US, once known as a global leader of press freedom, continues to stray further from its reputation, now trailing about 16 points behind its northern neighbor after falling by 2.87 points in the last 12 months.

From Canada to Nicaragua: A 54-Point Spread

While some countries in the Americas have seen minor improvements, these strides do not necessarily indicate strong press freedom conditions or significant regional growth.

In fact, only five countries in the Americas meet RSF's "satisfactory" range. The organization divides scores into five categories:

As displayed in the chart above, the scores for the countries across the Americas vary widely, with Nicaragua scoring in the 20s, placing it in what RSF refers to as the "very serious" range, while Canada sits at the top of the chart, just short of 80 points.

Very serious. Five countries in the Americas, including Cuba, Venezuela, and Nicaragua, remain classified as having "very serious" press freedom environments. Despite modest improvements in some, including Cuba and Nicaragua, this rank indicates continued harsh reporting environments.

Difficult. Nine countries have been listed in RSF’s "difficult" category. Of these countries, only Bolivia, Colombia, Guatemala and Honduras have improved since 2025, while the others have declined.

Problematic. Another nine countries, including the OECS bloc, were classified as problematic. RSF specifically singled out the decline in the US, given its previous reputation as a staunch defender of the First Amendment. Within this group, Uruguay and Brazil are the only countries that showed improvement in their scores.

Satisfactory. Only five countries in the Americas were classified as "satisfactory," with Canada and Jamaica having shown improvement. No country in the region met the standards for "good," which is RSF's highest score range.

The Region's Weakest Indicators

RSF uses five indicators to determine each country's press freedom score: economic, political, social, legal and safety context.

Despite the reputation for violence in parts of the Americas, particularly in Latin America, and RSFs continued concern for the safety of media workers, the indicator breakdown shows that the region's weakest indicators are, on average, related to the conditions journalists work under rather than the physical risks they face.

As shown in the above chart, economic context proved to be the Americas' weakest indicator on average, closely followed by political context. This aligns with RSF's concerns over the region's economic fragility and pressure from political actors, who the organization said have fueled hostile rhetoric towards journalists and implemented legal and administrative constraints, restrictions on information and weaponised legal systems to suppress journalism.

The United States, which RSF described as experiencing one of the most severe crises of press freedom in modern US history, is just one example of this broader trend of political pressure. The country has dropped 7 points in press freedom since 2022, a decline RSF attributes in part to the influence of Donald Trump, whose hostile rhetoric towards the press has been mirrored by political allies in countries like El Salvador and Argentina.

Verbal attacks from political leaders do not mean physical aggression has weakened, though. Countries like Mexico, Ecuador and Peru have long dealt with violence against their media workers, often stemming from organized crime. These issues are only worsened when compounded with other indicators like economic fragility and the pressures described above.

Ultimately, the decline in press freedom across the Americas cannot be attributed to one issue alone. Economic instability, political pressure, legal constraints and threats to journalists continue to overlap, weakening the ability of the press to operate freely and hold power accountable.
